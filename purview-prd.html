The 12-Team Purview PRD

Microsoft Purview's policy sync logic had drifted into 12 different shapes, one per product team. I wrote and aligned a single PRD to standardize it, from an engineering seat with no formal PM authority.

Role
Software Engineer (PM-grade work)
Company
Microsoft Purview
Scope
12 product teams
Skills
Stakeholder alignment, PRD authoring, technical design

The problem

Microsoft Purview is a suite of 12 security and compliance products, from Data Loss Prevention to eDiscovery to Records Management, each built by its own team. The policy sync workflow, the mechanism that keeps a policy's state consistent across the platform, had been implemented slightly differently by each of those teams. Customers reported sync and status-visibility issues. Engineers across teams had no shared standard to build against. There was no single owner of the workflow.

My task

I was a software engineer, not a PM, and the fix was not a code change in one service. It was an alignment problem across 12 teams that did not report to each other or to me. I took on standardizing the policy sync workflow end to end: defining one shared design and getting every team to commit to it.

What I did

  • Mapped the affected teams and their differences. I documented how each of the 12 teams handled sync today, so the conversation could start from facts rather than assumptions.
  • Surfaced conflicts before drafting, not after. Rather than writing a spec and defending it in a large review, I had 1:1 conversations with each team first. That is where the real disagreements lived: edge cases, ordering assumptions, ownership boundaries. I wanted those on the table before I wrote a word of the doc.
  • Authored the PRD. I drafted a single specification for a unified sync calculation that absorbed the legitimate needs each team had raised, and made explicit the trade-offs where teams had to converge.
  • Refactored the sync logic to align with the new design, so the spec was backed by working implementation rather than theory.
  • Coordinated end-to-end validation with solution teams across multiple geographic locations to confirm the design held across the platform.
12 → 1

Consolidated 12 separate per-team sync designs into one shared spec that every team committed to. The alignment itself, getting 12 independent teams to converge on a single workflow, was the deliverable I owned end to end.

Why it matters for product

This is the work that made me want to be a PM. It was not about authority; I had none. It was about reducing 12 conflicting realities into one shared decision by understanding each team's actual constraints, surfacing conflict early, and writing a spec people could commit to. That is the core of the job. It is also a direct answer to the fair question about a candidate moving from engineering to product: I have already done stakeholder management at scale, just from a different seat.

A note on honesty: I left Microsoft in April 2025, before the standardized workflow shipped. I therefore do not claim post-ship outcomes such as sync-issue reduction percentages or SLA deltas. The verifiable result is the alignment across all 12 teams, which is the part I owned.
