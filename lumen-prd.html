The Lumen Impact Founding PRD

Ideas do not ship products, specs do. I wrote the founding PRD that turned a vision into an executable plan: a scoped MVP, defined KPIs, research-grounded personas, and a phased roadmap.

Role
Co-founder, Product Manager (lead author)

The problem

Lumen Impact started as a business idea between me and my brother. But without a concrete artifact translating the idea into scope, success metrics, and a plan, we and any future contributors would build different things in parallel. I had already seen at Microsoft what happens when a shared workflow drifts into 12 versions. I was not going to let that happen on day one of my own company.

My task

Write a founding PRD that turned the vision into an executable product plan: scope the MVP, define success metrics, identify the target users, decompose features into workstreams, and commit to a roadmap.

What I did

I authored the founding PRD in November 2025, co-authored with my brother Gordon and an early collaborator, Millie Gao. It covered:

  • Goal grounded in first-principles research, not assumption, tied back to the donor study and competitive analysis.
  • Target-audience personas for both sides of the marketplace: donor and recipient.
  • MVP decomposed across 11 feature workstreams: user accounts, proximity matching, donation and request posts, search and filter, pickup coordination, payment, activity feed, admin dashboard, reporting and safety, notifications, and feedback for metrics.
  • KPIs: signups per week, items donated, requests fulfilled, average match latency, retention, and most active ZIP codes.
  • Success metrics ranked by research importance: ease of use first, then convenience, then cost, the same priorities the donor study surfaced.
  • A phased roadmap: Phase 1 responsive web app, Phase 2 mobile app.
  • The business decisions too, because a founder PRD cannot pretend they do not exist: an LLC formation comparison weighing five-year costs, brand-naming candidates, and a launch and promotion strategy.
11 workstreams

The PRD became the single source of truth for the MVP. Every product decision after it traced back to a specific section, which is exactly what a founding spec is supposed to buy you.

Why it matters for product

This is the second major PRD I have authored; the first was the 12-team Purview policy sync spec. The two together show the same instinct in very different settings: take ambiguity, impose structure, and give a team a document they can actually build against. Scoping an MVP, designing KPIs, decomposing a roadmap, and being explicit about what is in and out are the daily mechanics of the PM job, and this is direct evidence I can do them.
