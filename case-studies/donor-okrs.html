From Donor Research to OKRs

Before writing a line of code, I ran a 24-person study to learn what donors actually optimize for, then turned the findings into the success metrics that drove every product decision that followed.

Role
Co-founder, Product Manager
Company
Lumen Impact
Method
24-participant motivation study
Skills
Metric extraction, prioritization, OKRs

The problem

I co-founded Lumen Impact after my brother described being unable to find local people in need despite donation tools existing everywhere. Before building anything, I needed to know what donors actually optimize for when they decide whether to give, not what donation platforms assume they do. Without those success metrics, every prioritization call would be gut feel, and gut feel is how a two-person team wastes its only scarce resource: time.

My task

Design a study that would produce a concrete, ranked set of donor-priority metrics I could turn into OKRs and use to drive product prioritization.

What I did

  • Studied 24 donors across both the emotional and the practical drivers of giving, so the picture was not skewed toward one or the other.
  • Asked from a stance of being willing to be wrong, not to confirm a hypothesis I already held. The point was to find out what mattered, even if it contradicted my assumptions about the product.
  • Ranked what surfaced so the output was a prioritized set of metrics, not an undifferentiated list of nice-to-haves.
3 metrics

The study produced our three success metrics: ease of use, convenience, and cost. These became our OKRs and the input to every subsequent product decision, including the 30-person usability study that decomposed them into measurable sub-components.

The most useful part: what to defer

The study did not just tell me what to focus on. It told me what I could safely deprioritize without losing donors. As one example, tax incentives did not register as a motivator for the respondents, which meant I could push that entire surface area later instead of building it early. The combination of "here is what to focus on" and "here is what can wait" was the real output. It gave me a defensible prioritization framework before any code existed.

Why it matters for product

Anyone can list features. The harder, more valuable skill is deriving the small set of metrics that should govern the roadmap, and being disciplined about what they let you ignore. This study is how Lumen Impact had OKRs on day one, and it fed directly into the usability work that came next.
