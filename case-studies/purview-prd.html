The 12-Team Purview PRD

Microsoft Purview's policy sync logic had drifted into 12 different shapes, one per product team. I wrote and aligned a single PRD to standardize it, from an engineering seat with no formal PM authority.

Role
Software Engineer (PM-grade work)
Company
Microsoft Purview
Scope
12 product teams
Skills
Stakeholder alignment, PRD authoring, technical design

The problem

Microsoft Purview is a suite of 12 security and compliance products, from Data Loss Prevention to eDiscovery to Records Management, each built by its own team. The policy sync workflow, the mechanism that keeps a policy's state consistent across the platform, had been implemented slightly differently by each of those teams. Customers reported sync and status-visibility issues. Engineers across teams had no shared standard to build against. There was no single owner of the workflow.

My task

Standardize the policy sync workflow across all 12 product teams: define one shared design and gett each team to commit to it.

What I did

  • Find the correct product owners. Before I could start my research process, I utilized my existing resources and network to find the leads and managers for each product team through the help of my PM, my manager, and my senior engineer.
  • Do my own product research. I reached out to each of the 12 teams to get access to their code and documentation. Before I initiated any meetings, I wanted to understand their user stories, ecosystem, and product requirements to maximize efficiency of everyone's time.
  • Surfaced conflicts before drafting, not after. Rather than writing a spec and defending it in a large review, I met in 1:1 conversations with individual product teams first. That is where the real disagreements lived: edge cases, ordering assumptions, ownership boundaries. I wanted those on the table before I wrote a word of the PRD doc.
  • Authored the PRD. I drafted a single specification for a unified sync calculation that absorbed the legitimate needs each team had raised, and made explicit the trade-offs where teams had to converge.
  • Coordinated end-to-end validation and migration efforts with solution teams across multiple geographic locations to confirm the design held across the platform and maintained quality and consistency for the existing users.
12 → 1

Consolidated 12 separate per-team sync designs into one shared spec that every team committed to. The alignment itself, getting 12 independent teams to converge on a single workflow, was the deliverable I owned end to end.

Why it matters for product

There were 2 main lessons from this project:

One. Influence without authority. This is the project that made me want to be a PM. I was a junior software engineer taking on PM work, and the project leads were my stakeholders. I had no authority. Only when they saw the value I brought to their interest (reducing ambiguity and work on their plates) did they start sharing product insight and approve my spec. Earning that trust one stakeholder at a time was my first real lesson in stakeholder management.

Two. Cross-functional PM work. The core of the work was turning 12 conflicting realities into one shared decision. I learned each team's actual constraints, surfaced conflicts early, and wrote a spec people could commit to. The technical depth mattered, but the harder skill was communicating across solution teams and translating their requirements into a roadmap and documentation everyone could build from.
