The 30-Person Usability Study

A mixed-method study that took three abstract donor-priority metrics and broke them into measurable sub-components, turning "we believe this is good UX" into a framework we could actually decide with.

Role
Co-founder, Product Manager
Company
Lumen Impact
Method
Mixed-method, 30 participants
Skills
Research design, metrics decomposition

The problem

After shipping Lumen Impact's first website design, the team had qualitative confidence in the direction but no rigorous test of whether the experience actually moved the things donors said they cared about. An earlier 24-person study had surfaced our top three donor-prioritized metrics, ease of use, convenience, and cost, but those were abstract. "Ease of use" is not something you can directly observe or improve. The design had not been validated against any of them.

My task

Validate the website design against the three donor-priority metrics in a way that produced concrete, sub-component-level signal, not just "users liked it" or "users did not." I wanted output specific enough to drive the next round of product decisions.

What I did

  • Decomposed each metric into measurable sub-components. Instead of trying to measure "ease of use" as one thing, I broke each of the three top-level metrics into a small set of specific, observable properties of the experience. Each metric became something I could actually test.
  • Built a defined task flow. Every participant walked through the same set of end-to-end product moments, so I was comparing responses to the same experience rather than to scattered impressions.
  • Chose a mixed-method design on purpose. I ran 3 group moderated sessions of 3 to 4 participants each, plus 19 self-moderated guided sessions. The group sessions surfaced shared friction points and the language people used; the self-moderated 1:1 sessions captured individual behavior without facilitator bias. Together they covered both the "what do people say as a group" and "what do people do alone" angles.
  • Collected component-level ratings. After each task, participants rated the relevant sub-components, so every score tied back to a specific moment in the product.
30 participants

3 moderated group sessions plus 19 self-moderated guided sessions. The result: each of the three abstract donor metrics became a set of measurable sub-components, and we could see exactly which UX elements moved which metric.

The outcome

The study translated three abstract donor-priority metrics into a concrete product-decision framework. From that point forward, every product decision could be evaluated against a measurable component of the metric framework rather than gut feel. It resolved the exact problem a small team runs into constantly: "we believe this is good UX, but we cannot prove it moves the things our users care about."

Why it matters for product

This is the research instinct that consumer and marketplace teams care about most: not running a study for its own sake, but designing one whose output is decision-grade. The metrics decomposition is the transferable skill. Give me a fuzzy goal and I will turn it into something a team can measure and act on.
